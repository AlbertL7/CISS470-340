üèõÔ∏èFIPS 199: The Foundation You Must Understand First

üö® MANDATORY: FIPS 199 is a Federal Information Processing Standard - it's LAW for federal agencies. NIST 800-60 shows you HOW to implement it.

FIPS 199 Security Objectives (The CIA Triad)

Security Objective Definition Question to Ask
üîê Confidentiality Preserving authorized restrictions on information access and disclosure "What happens if unauthorized people see this?"
‚úÖ Integrity Guarding against improper information modification or destruction "What happens if this data is changed or deleted?"
‚ö° Availability Ensuring timely and reliable access to information "What happens if we can't access this when needed?"

The FIPS 199 Formula

Security Category (SC) = {(confidentiality, impact), (integrity, impact), (availability, impact)}

Where impact is LOW, MODERATE, or HIGH

Potential Impact Definitions (FIPS 199)

Impact Level FIPS 199 Definition Real-World Translation
LOW Limited adverse effect on organizational operations, assets, or individuals Inconvenience, minor financial loss, minor damage to reputation
MODERATE Serious adverse effect on organizational operations, assets, or individuals Significant degradation in mission, significant financial loss, significant harm to individuals
HIGH Severe or catastrophic adverse effect on organizational operations, assets, or individuals Mission failure, major financial loss, loss of life, severe harm to individuals

Step 4: Adjust for Special Factors

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: The 800-60 recommendations are starting points! You must consider your specific context.

Detailed Special Factors Analysis

üìä Factor 1: Aggregation

Definition: When large volumes of low-impact data combine to create higher impact

Example: Customer Records

  • 1 customer record leaked = embarrassing (LOW)
  • 100 records leaked = reportable breach (MODERATE)
  • 1 million records leaked = class action lawsuit, regulatory fines (HIGH)

Rule of Thumb: If breach affects >5,000 individuals ‚Üí Consider MODERATE minimum
If breach affects >100,000 individuals ‚Üí Consider HIGH

üè• Factor 2: Life Safety

Definition: Information that could impact human life or safety if compromised

Examples That Trigger HIGH:

  • Emergency response systems (911 dispatch)
  • Medical device configurations
  • Industrial control systems (SCADA)
  • Transportation safety systems
  • Witness protection information

Rule: If integrity or availability loss could cause injury/death ‚Üí Automatic HIGH

üí∞ Factor 3: Financial Impact

Definition: Direct financial loss or liability from compromise

Financial Impact Typical Adjustment
< $10,000 No adjustment needed
$10,000 - $100,000 Consider MODERATE minimum
$100,000 - $1 million MODERATE required
> $1 million HIGH strongly recommended

üîê Factor 4: Privacy Information

Definition: Information protected by privacy laws and regulations

Regulatory Minimums:

  • Privacy Act Data: MODERATE minimum for Confidentiality
  • HIPAA PHI: MODERATE minimum, often HIGH
  • Student Records (FERPA): MODERATE typical
  • Financial (GLBA): MODERATE minimum
  • Children's Data (COPPA): HIGH recommended

üè¢ Factor 5: Mission Criticality

Definition: How essential the information is to organizational mission

Questions to Ask:

  • Can the organization function without this for 1 hour? (If no ‚Üí HIGH Availability)
  • Would corruption cause mission failure? (If yes ‚Üí HIGH Integrity)
  • Is this our "secret sauce"? (If yes ‚Üí HIGH Confidentiality)

‚è∞ Factor 6: Time Sensitivity

Definition: How the value/impact changes over time

Information Age Impact Adjustment Example
Real-time critical HIGH Availability Stock trading data
Daily relevance MODERATE Availability Daily reports
Historical/Archive LOW Availability 5-year old records
Special Factor Consideration Potential Adjustment
Aggregation Large volumes increase impact Low ‚Üí Moderate or High
Life Safety Could affect human life Any ‚Üí High
Financial Impact Major financial consequences Moderate ‚Üí High
Privacy Act Data Contains Privacy Act information Low ‚Üí Moderate (minimum)
Trade Secrets Competitive advantage at risk Moderate ‚Üí High

NIST 800-70: Security Configuration Baselines

üîß What is NIST 800-70?

NIST 800-70 provides guidance on implementing security configuration baselines through standardized checklists. It bridges the gap between selecting controls (800-53) and actually configuring your systems securely.

The Domino Effect of Categorization

üéØ Example: How a HIGH Category Cascades Through All Standards

  1. 800-60: Healthcare records = HIGH integrity (patient safety)
  2. FIPS 200: Must implement all 17 families at maximum level
  3. 800-53: Must implement ~350+ security controls
  4. 800-70: Must apply most restrictive security baselines (DISA STIGs Level I)
  5. 800-30: Risk assessments quarterly (not annually)
  6. 800-53A: Continuous monitoring required (not periodic)
  7. 800-37: ATO valid for only 1 year (not 3 years)

Result: One categorization decision affects EVERYTHING!

‚ö†Ô∏èCommon Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

‚ùå Pitfall: Under-categorization

Problem: Rating everything as LOW to avoid security requirements

Solution: Remember that a breach will cost far more than prevention. Be honest about impacts.

‚ùå Pitfall: Over-categorization

Problem: Rating everything as HIGH "just to be safe"

Solution: This wastes resources. Use 800-60 guidance and document your rationale.

‚ùå Pitfall: Ignoring Aggregation

Problem: Not considering that large volumes increase impact

Solution: 1 record = LOW, but 1 million records might = HIGH

‚ùå Pitfall: Missing Information Types

Problem: Incomplete inventory leads to gaps

Solution: Use data discovery tools and interview all stakeholders

‚ùå Pitfall: Static Categorization

Problem: Not updating when system changes

Solution: Review categorization annually and after major changes

‚ùå Pitfall: Availability Blind Spot

Problem: Focus only on C and I, ignore A

Solution: Consider business continuity and life safety impacts
