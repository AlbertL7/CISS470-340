üìñ Introduction: Tom's Challenge to Nathan

By John Hermans (KPMG Cyber Lead Partner EMEA) and Ton Diemont (KPMG Senior Manager)

"So give me the right guidelines for how to treat cyber risk and bring cybersecurity back to basics."

‚Äî CEO Tom to Chief Risk Officer Nathan

Cybersecurity has been in the spotlight for the past few years. Due to the number and seriousness of cyber incidents, the media's focus on such incidents, and the importance of tackling cyber issues in the extensive digitization of most organizations, this area requires the attention of directors and managers everywhere.

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Challenge: But it needs to be tackled in the appropriate way and with the required subtlety, as a component of integral risk management.

The fact that cybersecurity is important to every organization needs no further explanation. On an almost daily basis, various incidents demonstrate how great the risks are and that individual hackers and professionally organized cybercriminals are extremely active.

The Complexity Problem

To many, however, this is not a simple task because the world of cybersecurity seems elusive due to:

  • üîß Its specialist character
  • üí¨ Technical jargon used
  • üéØ Difficulty distinguishing primary from secondary issues
  • üì∞ Media coverage contributing to culture of fear

Almost no distinction is made between imposters on eBay, hackers who crash websites, and organized criminal gangs using systematic strategies to steal company secrets (crown jewels). Such distinctions are extremely important because not all organizations are equally attractive to different types of cybercriminals.

‚úÖ The Solution: It is truly essential that heads of organizations themselves actively lead the crusade for cybersecurity. Within the complexity, leaders need to consider relevant issues soundly and, at the very least, pose the right questions.

üéØ Treating Cybersecurity Risk with Proper Nuance

The seriousness of cybersecurity risks means that cybersecurity does require boardroom attention‚Äîbut in the appropriate context. Organizations need to avoid panicked responses that have not been thought through.

The Media Problem

The media regularly paint a dramatic picture of cybersecurity as if numerous organizations are helpless victims of cybercriminals. Moreover, all types of crime are lumped together, causing anxiety among organizations that is not based on facts.

üìä Different Profiles for Different Organizations

Small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) have a completely different profile than multinationals, and an SME need have few worries about many of the incidents reported in the media.

The Truth is More Nuanced

The reality:

  • ‚úÖ Risks are certainly controllable
  • ‚úÖ Cybercriminals are not invincible geniuses
  • ‚úÖ Government and enterprises have significant knowledge to fight cybercrime
‚ö†Ô∏è Important Reality Check: We need to realize that 100% security is an illusion and that the pursuit of total security will lead not only to frustration but also possibly to a false sense of security.

Business as Usual Approach

We ought to start considering cybersecurity as "business as usual," as a theme that deserves attention in much the same way as the risk of fire or fraud. These are themes tackled by management in a structural way, from a risk-management perspective, with defenses and responses not founded on building a completely watertight system.

üîÑ The New Perspective

Many organizations need to examine cybersecurity differently. They should not take decisions based on fear of what's happening outside, but reason from:

  • Their own strengths
  • Awareness of risks run by their own organization
  • Accordance with the organization's risk profile
  • Their specific nature

The starting point of the exploration of an organization's cyber risk is the determination of that organization's risk profile and risk appetite.

Key Questions for Risk Profile

  • üéØ "How interesting is the organization to potential cyber criminals?"
  • üîó "How dependent is the organization on the services of other organizations?"
  • ‚öñÔ∏è "How much risk is the organization willing to accept?"

üìä Determining the Cyber Risk Profile

In order to determine an organization's cyber risk profile, we need to use a model that covers the following five aspects:

1

Context & Environment

What is the organization's internal and external context? In which markets is it active? How dependent on digitization? How linked to other organizations that could form additional risk?

2

Targets

What could be relevant intended targets within the organization, and also within the chain in which the organization is active?

3

Threat Actors

To which group of cybercriminals, and why, is the organization an attractive target? Which resources could the attacker deploy?

4

Vulnerabilities & Resilience

Which vulnerabilities could cybercriminals exploit? Not only technical but also human actions. What is the level of resilience? How fast can the organization recover?

5

Regulatory Requirements

What are the regulatory and legislative requirements with regard to cybersecurity that pertain to the organization?

üí° Key Outcome: Based on analysis of these five aspects, an organization can determine its risk profile, the amount of risk it's willing to accept (risk appetite), and implement the appropriate set of cybersecurity measures.
‚ö†Ô∏è Remember: It will never be possible to achieve 100% security, so there is no point in pursuing such an aim!

üíä Treating Cyber Risk

Cyber risk management programs must consider an organization's risk appetite. Specific cyber risks can and must be treated by applying necessary measures and reacting effectively when subjected to cyber attack.

Key Considerations for Treatment

üëë Focus on Your Crown Jewels

In view of the fact that it is impossible to protect everything, cybersecurity requires special attention regarding the protection of the organization's most valued information. It is therefore vital that an organization specify its crown jewels that need to be protected.

üë• Humans Remain the Weakest Link

It is essential to have technical systems to protect, identify intruders, and respond to attacks, but human beings are actually the weakest link in many organizations. However, humans may also be the best asset in the organization's defense, if they are properly informed and trained.

üîç Complementing Preventative with Detective Measures

Whereas organizations once primarily relied on preventative measures to avoid cybersecurity incidents, attention is increasingly being paid to detection of attacks, to enable immediate and appropriate response. We see growing use of technical monitoring facilities to detect and analyze alien traffic.

üöÄ Focus on Organization's Capability to Respond

We believe it is unfortunately only a question of time before an organization becomes a victim of a cyber incident. Instead of being helpless, an organization can prepare for serious attacks. It's vital to include processing of cyber incidents in crisis plans, including formulation of communication protocols during incidents.

ü§ù Cooperation Is Essential

Besides being able to respond to incidents, it's crucial for organizations to remain up to date and informed of emerging threats, and learn from other organizations. Various organizations exist to help:

  • National level (National Cyber Security Centre)
  • Sector level (International Sharing and Analysis Centers - ISACs)
  • Informal cooperative associations (CISO groups within industries)

Remember: An incident at another organization is also a potential threat to one's own organization.

üîÑ Alignment of Cyber Risk Treatment

Technology alone is not the answer to cybersecurity issues. The answer lies in an integrated approach to cyber risk treatment, focusing on both softer elements (governance, culture, behavior) and harder ones (technology).

Integrated Approach Components

üéØ Leadership and Governance

Organization leaders need to demonstrate, in word and deed, that they regard themselves as owners of cybersecurity, showing they intend to manage associated risks adequately.

üë• Human Behavior

Cybersecurity involves not only appropriate technical measures but also creation of a culture where people are alert to and aware of ways they can contribute to security.

üìä Information Risk Management

An adequate approach to all-embracing and effective risk management regarding information provision, also in relation to partner organizations.

üîÑ Business Continuity & Crisis Management

Good preparation for possible incidents and ability to minimize their impact. Involves crisis and stakeholder management.

‚öôÔ∏è Operations and Technology

Implementation of checks and control measures to identify cybersecurity risks and minimize impact of incidents.

‚öñÔ∏è Legislation

Complying with legislation regarding information protection.

Benefits of Holistic Model

  • ‚úÖ Minimization of risk and consequences of cyber attacks
  • ‚úÖ Better decisions in cybersecurity field
  • ‚úÖ Clear lines of communication on cybersecurity
  • ‚úÖ Contribution to better reputation
  • ‚úÖ Enhancement of knowledge and competences
  • ‚úÖ Benchmarking against peers

To continuously manage and mitigate cyber risks, organizations need to address these risks in a flexible and ongoing manner. This requires a central and overarching perspective on risks requiring treatment and management attention.

üíº Business as Usual Approaches

1. Integration into Enterprise Risk Management

Cyber risk management should be linked to/integrated in the enterprise risk management (ERM) of an organization. Despite the topic and specific expertise, the approach must be fully aligned to existing risk management processes.

Benefits:

  • Supports consistent risk language and classification
  • Better and more quickly understood by stakeholders
  • Enables top-down approach where IRM and ERM are aligned
  • Shows added value by providing insight into opportunities and risks

2. Three Lines of Defense Model

First Line

Primary Business

Cybersecurity risk should be a primary business responsibility, not solely IT. Should be part of annual performance objectives of senior management.

Second Line

Support Functions

Multidisciplinary team of risk managers, security officers, compliance, legal, and HR representatives providing balanced views.

Third Line

Independent Audit

Audit as the independent observer creating full transparent views and supporting appropriate steering and decisions.

3. Managing with Predefined Risk Appetite

Many organizations have a "Pavlovian response" to immediately start drafting controls when risks are identified, without asking what level of risk is acceptable. These decisions are often made by cyber risk professionals without consulting business representatives.

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Point: The starting point of managing cyber risks is defining the organization's risk appetite‚Äîan exercise to be performed by business representatives, not cybersecurity specialists.

Follows ISO 31000:2009, Risk Management‚ÄîPrinciples and Guidelines. Organizations increasingly formalize risk appetite statements containing both quantitative and qualitative components.

Note on Quantification: The qualitative components are most applicable for cyber risk because quantifying cyber risk impact is still based on empiric and rough estimations, not proven quantitative methodology. We lack historical, actuarial data despite efforts by organizations like the World Economic Forum.

4. Using Embedded Risk Management Processes

When cyber is assessed as relevant, it can be assessed and treated by senior management and experienced specialists. The cyber risk assessment process (identify, analyze, evaluate) should be identical to that used for ERM.

ALE Methodology: Annualized Loss Expectancy can assist in defining cyber risk appetite by expressing all losses as expected annual loss, providing consistent approach and evaluation.

Question: Does setting risk appetite differ between small and large organizations?

Answer: No, basically not. Most likely the thresholds for analyzing and evaluating risks differ, as larger organizations potentially have larger financial buffers and higher appetite for risk.

5. Treatment Options Aligned with ISO 31000

Traditional risk treatment techniques only treat risk as "threats" (negative consequences). Modern treatment also addresses opportunities (positive consequences). Appropriate combined treatment options are not mutually exclusive and should be aligned with ISO 31000:2009:

  1. Avoiding the activity that gives rise to the risk
  2. Taking or increasing the risk in order to pursue an opportunity
  3. Removing the risk source
  4. Changing the likelihood
  5. Changing the consequences
  6. Sharing the risk with other parties (e.g., risk financing, contracts)
  7. Retaining the risk by informed decision
üí∞ Cost-Benefit Balance: Prioritized risk treatment options should be aligned with existing organization objectives and strategies so there's only a need to spend money once. If certain investments must be postponed, the organization should follow a formal temporary risk assessment and well-balanced acceptance process.

Impact Criteria Beyond Financial

A key element in supporting unification is ensuring that next to financial impact criteria, the non-financial impact criteria are also formalized:

  • Regulatory impacts
  • Customer impacts
  • Legal consequences
  • Compliance issues
  • Operational disruption
  • Staff impacts
  • Reputational damage

These should preferably be linked to financial criteria, meaning severe non-financial cyber occurrence is treated equally as risk with high financial impact.

üéì Conclusion

The following cyber risk management statement represents those organization capabilities CEO and board expect to be demonstrated in terms of treating cyber risks:

üìã TREATING CYBER RISKS - Organization Capability Statement

The organization's risk treatment capabilities align with its risk profile, risk appetite and context. Risk treatment methodology is not reinvented for cyber risks but is a subset of the ERM system. Risk treatment covers all cyber risk sources, likelihoods, and impacts.

Risk sources include supply chain, cloud, mobile devices, and social media. Impacts are either noninsurable in nature, or insurable in part or whole, and may take various forms (such as fines, reputational damage, loss of customers, loss of employees, and stock devaluation).

Impact management preparations are required for insurable risks, crisis management, forensics investigation, customer notification, and business interruption. Cyber risk treatment is prioritized, reiterative, and cyclical. Risk owners complete risk and control action plans that balance threat with opportunity to organization objectives and consider cost-benefit.

Appropriate combined treatment options are not mutually exclusive, are appropriate to the case in hand, and are aligned with ISO 31000:2009, Risk Management‚ÄîPrinciples and Guidelines by: (1) avoiding the activity that gives rise to the risk; (2) taking or increasing the risk in order to pursue an opportunity; (3) removing the risk source; (4) changing the likelihood; (5) changing the consequences; (6) sharing the risk with other parties (e.g., risk financing, contracts); and (7) retaining the risk by informed decision.

Cyber risk management should be "business as usual" - integrated into enterprise risk management, not treated as a specialized IT concern.
