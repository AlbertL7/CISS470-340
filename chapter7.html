📖 Introduction: Nathan's Warning to CEO Tom

By Steve Durbin, Managing Director, Information Security Forum Ltd.

"To say that cybersecurity presents complex challenges is an understatement. The scope of risk to sensitive information has grown exponentially during the twenty-first century. Those risks not only involve technical factors, but human, cultural, and legal factors, as well as economics. Of course, the profession of cybersecurity has struggled to grow in tandem with these challenges. But nobody has the resources to ensure complete data security. Figuring out where security investments are justified requires a sophisticated understanding of the risk landscape."

— Nathan, Chief Risk Officer

This chapter explores how organizations can develop that sophisticated understanding through structured risk assessment methodologies, cultural awareness, and strategic prioritization of security investments.

🌐 The Landscape of Risk

Hardly a day goes by when the evening news does not include a report about a major institution reluctantly announcing that its files have been hacked. The stories tend to follow a familiar pattern:

😔 Official Regret

Expressions of deep regret and apology

🛡️ Reassurance

Attempts to reassure customers and stakeholders

🤝 Pledges

Promises to prevent future occurrences

The New Normal

Attacks on institutional and corporate databases have become the new normal. A generation of workers comfortable with information sharing has also grown accustomed to its negative consequences. The capabilities of cybercriminals continue advancing at an alarming pace.

⚠️ The Cost of Complacency: Losses associated with major data attacks run into the millions and are increasingly seen as just another cost of doing business.

A Welcome Development

There is a growing understanding of these consequences. A movement in the leadership ranks of both business and government agencies to manage cyber risks more effectively and improve resilience has followed. Until fairly recently, most senior managers and board members regarded cybersecurity as essentially a technical problem for their IT departments—not as an existential issue requiring greater investment and engagement throughout the organization.

Technology Vulnerabilities

Technology flaws create opportunities for attackers to infiltrate an organization's technical infrastructure:

  • 🔧 Design flaws in system architecture
  • 🔐 Weak or broken encryption
  • 📝 Insufficient event logging
  • 💻 Software malfunction and bugs

💡 Key Insight: Understanding and realistically assessing the vulnerabilities of an organization's system components is essential. But it is people, far more than technology, that present the greatest risks.

👥 The People Factor

Most high-profile attacks originate outside of victimized organizations—often from halfway around the world. But the network openings that allow cyber attackers to burrow in, infect databases, and potentially take down an organization's file servers, overwhelmingly originate with trusted insiders.

Three Categories of Insider Threats

😈

Malicious

Driven by intent to enrich themselves through sale of sensitive data or retaliate for perceived slights

🎭

Deceptive

Social engineering used to trick employees into divulging proprietary codes, passwords, or information

😰

Inadvertent

Accidental behavior by insiders without any intention of harming their employer (most common)

📊 ISF Survey Finding: According to a survey of Information Security Forum members, the vast majority of network openings were created innocently through accidental or inadvertent behavior by insiders without any intention of harming their employer. In many cases, vulnerability was the ironic result of a trusted employee doing something seemingly ordinary like taking files home to work on in their spare time.

Mobile Device Risks

That risk is exacerbated by personal mobile devices. Welcome or not, they have become inexorably tied into corporate information systems. Their use frequently reflects their owners' relaxed consumer habits.

⚠️ The Cost Differential:
  • Individual Impact: Limited to personal embarrassment
  • Corporate Impact: Can be huge - marketing plans, product roadmaps, pricing strategies, personnel records, customer data, confidential correspondence

EU GDPR Impact

🇪🇺 General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)

For anyone operating using personal information relating to European citizens, the stakes have been significantly raised:

  • Penalties up to 4% of global turnover
  • Comes into force in 2018
  • Adds complexity and potential cost to critical information asset management
  • Applies regardless of organization location

As a result, there is now a much clearer recognition of both the immediate and longer-term costs associated with cyber attacks. Identifying an organization's vulnerabilities is key to developing effective responses.

📊 A Structured Approach to Assessing and Managing Risk

There are different tools and software available in the market to help risk managers assess their information vulnerabilities. Symantec, Trend Micro, NetIQ, ISF, and others are reputable firms with applications and techniques focused on different aspects of risk management.

💡 Key Principle: A robust information risk assessment methodology should not simply be a piece of software but a comprehensive procedure to identify, analyze, evaluate, and manage a variety of information risks throughout organizations in a structured manner, then generate risk profiles in terms meaningful to the organization's business.

The ISF IRAM2 Methodology

The Information Security Forum's second-generation Information Risk Assessment Methodology (IRAM2) identifies six distinct phases of risk management:

1

Integrated View

Generate an integrated view of information risk, ranging from business processes through to technology

2

Business Impact

Realistically assess worst-case scenarios—the potential business impact if information assets become compromised

3

Threat Mapping

Map different types of threats, both malicious and accidental, that could potentially affect the business

4

Vulnerability Assessment

Assess vulnerabilities to different threat events and the strength of any controls already in place

5

Risk Evaluation

Evaluate the organization's risk appetite and likelihood of a successful threat in light of previous findings

6

Practical Approaches

Develop practical approaches to addressing the information risks which have been identified

In essence, it provides companies with a highly structured and practical method for assessing risks to guide critical business decisions. By taking a comprehensive view of risk exposure and focusing attention on the most significant ones, organizations may predict and prevent attacks instead of simply reacting to them after they occur.

Cultural Considerations

One of the most significant factors is the organization's culture, which often mirrors the society where it is located. For multinational organizations, this means taking into account:

  • 🌍 Different cultural views on protecting data
  • 📱 Responses to directives about safeguarding digital devices
  • 💼 Attitudes toward commingling corporate and personal business on devices

Study Findings

Cisco Study (decade ago)

Found risky behavior and rampant disregard for organization security policies occurred at alarming rates among employees in all parts of the world, though some were worse than others.

PwC Survey 2016 - Encouraging News

  • 65% of organizations collaborate to improve cybersecurity
  • 69% use cloud-based cybersecurity services
  • Many follow risk-based cybersecurity frameworks, most frequently ISO 27001

Generational Differences

👨‍💼 Generation Y Perspective:

As a whole, do not feel requirements for securing sensitive information apply to them as much as to senior colleagues. The prevailing view: it's the organization's job to make information secure—not the individual's.

Executive Awareness Gaps

Nasdaq Survey Findings

Highlights alarming gaps between awareness and accountability at the highest levels:

  • Too many board members and executives unable to understand security briefings
  • Unwilling to accept responsibility for data breaches
  • The more senior someone is, the less aware they tend to be of data security needs
  • Some senior executives think they're immune from security threats

Educational Origins

Most of today's employees have gone through educational institutions where they are encouraged to share information. Universities provide ready access to information sources across the country. But while we encourage people to share information, we don't teach security considerations around information use.

Result: When students become employees, they are thrust into a completely different, and far less forgiving, information security environment.

⚖️ Regulatory Compliance

In highly regulated environments, like financial services, there are numerous statutory requirements organizations need to comply with. This has tended to drive institutional behavior—they're compliant because they have to be.

⚠️ The Compliance Trap: If you are going to avoid innovative attacks, compliance will not necessarily help. A compliance-driven strategy might satisfy the authorities, but it will do little to discourage a creative hacker.

Compliance as a Starting Point

There are banks and other organizations that see compliance only as a starting point. That is because:

  • 📅 Regulations are outdated: By the time a regulation goes into effect, it is usually out of date
  • ⏮️ Backward-looking: Compliance regulations prevent past acts from repeating
  • 🎖️ Fighting old wars: Like preparing today to win a previous war

Why Cyber is Different

Cyber is different than other types of risk:

  • ⚡ Exceptionally fast moving - Unlike conventional risks
  • 🆕 Unprecedented attacks - Many have never been seen before
  • 🎯 Innovative threats - Constantly evolving attack vectors
✅ The Balancing Act:
  1. You really do have to be compliant
  2. You have to do everything reasonable to protect mission-critical information

📈 Maturing Security

As recently as 10 years ago, cybersecurity was primarily a function of IT departments. Organizations treated data protection as a technology matter—one best left to techies. Risk managers focused on traditional insurance coverages. Product line managers rarely felt any connection to the cybersecurity function.

🔄 That is all starting to change.

Levels of Cybersecurity Maturity

Medium Maturity

🎯 Prioritizing Protection

The Evolution of Security Focus

Years ago: Securing information was synonymous with safeguarding computers.

Today: The explosion of devices, users, and interconnection channels makes it essential to shift from device focus to information focus.

The New Paradigm

With so many devices and users touching the data, protecting them all is impossible. Instead, organizations need to focus on what is important from a business standpoint:

🛡️ Protecting Mission-Critical Information

Regardless of who might want to access it

📱 Device Agnostic

Irrespective of the devices they might be using

🌍 Location Independent

No matter where they might be coming from

This represents a sea change from earlier device-centric safeguards. Today, the approach to security begins and ends with the organization's data.

Key Questions for Data Protection

Essential Questions to Ask:

  • ❓ How do I protect my data?
  • ❓ Which data is truly mission critical?
  • ❓ Who really needs to access it?
  • ❓ What are my organization's crown jewels?
  • ❓ How do I need to protect them?
  • ❓ What behaviors am I trying to protect against?
  • ❓ Who actually needs this information, and when?

The Moving Parts of Security

Safeguarding an organization's mission-critical information is a process involving numerous moving parts—all subject to change over time:

Technology
Leadership
Culture
Policy
Environment

Adversaries who want to attack those assets are constantly on the lookout for any opportunities those changes create.

📊 Essential Actions: Regularly and systematically assess your organization's technological defenses, potential business impact scenarios, matrix of threats, and the resilience of current configuration to potential attacks. All are essential to developing a pragmatic plan to resist and, if worse comes to worst, to recover from an assault on your most critical data.

🎓 Conclusion

The following cyber risk management statement represents those organization capabilities CEO and board expect to be demonstrated in terms of identifying, analyzing, and evaluating (i.e., assessing) cyber risks:

📋 IDENTIFYING, ANALYZING, AND EVALUATING CYBER RISKS

The organization realistically assesses the vulnerabilities of its digital system components not just for technology flaws (such as in design, encryption, event logging or software malfunction) but for human factors. Trusted insiders present the highest risk (motivated either by malice or more commonly by accident) as well as third-party contractors, vendors, or temporary workers (essentially privileged users).

The organization commits to a robust and structured approach to assessing and managing risk and an information risk assessment methodology. This involves a six-part approach to:

  1. (1) generating an integrated view of information risk;
  2. (2) realistically assessing worst case;
  3. (3) mapping different types of threats, both malicious and accidental;
  4. (4) assessing vulnerabilities to different threat events and the strength of any controls already in place;
  5. (5) evaluating risk appetite and likelihood of a successful threat; and
  6. (6) developing practical approaches to addressing the information risks that have been identified.

Other factors examined include organization capability, security culture, commitment, people competence, user privilege patterns, technology, leadership, policy, and environment. There is a balance between regulatory compliance and doing everything reasonable to protect mission-critical information.

Cybersecurity maturity avoids barriers separating data security from the organization's core business functions and does not rely on device-centric safeguards. The focus begins and ends with the organization's data: how it is protected, which data is truly mission critical, what behaviors need to be protected against, and who really needs to access it and when.

🎯 The Bottom Line

Focus on protecting mission-critical information, not just devices. Build a structured approach to risk assessment that considers both technical and human factors.
