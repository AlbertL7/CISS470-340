Understanding FIPS 199: Purpose and Foundation Part 1 of 6

üìú Why FIPS 199 Matters

Historical Context

The E-Government Act of 2002 recognized information security as critical to U.S. economic and national security interests. Title III, the Federal Information Security Management Act (FISMA), tasked NIST with developing standards for categorizing information and systems based on risk levels.

üéØ Three Key FISMA Tasks for NIST

Task 1: Categorization Standards (FIPS 199) ‚ñ∂

Develop standards for all federal agencies to categorize information and systems based on providing appropriate security levels according to risk.

This is what FIPS 199 addresses! It provides the framework for expressing security needs consistently across government.
Task 2: Guidelines for Categories ‚ñ∂

Recommend types of information and systems to include in each category.

Addressed in subsequent NIST publications.

Task 3: Security Requirements ‚ñ∂

Define minimum security requirements (management, operational, technical controls) for each category.

Addressed in NIST SP 800-53 and related documents.

üèõÔ∏è Why Categorization Matters

Benefits of Common Framework:

  • Effective management and oversight of security programs
  • Coordination across civilian, defense, homeland security, and law enforcement
  • Consistent reporting to OMB and Congress
  • Common language for discussing security needs
  • Risk-based resource allocation

‚ö†Ô∏è Scope and Applicability

Applies to: All federal information and systems EXCEPT:

  • Classified information (handled under Executive Orders)
  • National security systems (as defined in 44 USC 3542(b)(2))

Effective: Upon Secretary of Commerce approval (February 2004)

Optional for: State/local/tribal governments and private sector critical infrastructure

üí° Key Understanding: FIPS 199 creates a common language for expressing security needs based on potential impact to the organization's mission, assets, and individuals.
The CIA Triad: Three Security Objectives Part 2 of 6

üîê Understanding Security Objectives

Foundation Concept

FISMA defines three fundamental security objectives that form the basis for all security categorization. These are universally known as the "CIA Triad".

üîí

Confidentiality

"Preserving authorized restrictions on information access and disclosure"

‚úì

Integrity

"Guarding against improper information modification or destruction"

‚ö°

Availability

"Ensuring timely and reliable access to and use of information"

üìñ Detailed Definitions and Loss Scenarios

üîí Confidentiality ‚ñ∂
Full Definition:

"Preserving authorized restrictions on information access and disclosure, including means for protecting personal privacy and proprietary information" [44 U.S.C., Sec. 3542]

Loss of Confidentiality = Unauthorized disclosure of information
Examples of Confidentiality Loss:
  • Customer data exposed in a breach
  • Proprietary information leaked to competitors
  • Medical records accessed without authorization
  • Financial data disclosed inappropriately
‚úì Integrity ‚ñ∂
Full Definition:

"Guarding against improper information modification or destruction, and includes ensuring information non-repudiation and authenticity" [44 U.S.C., Sec. 3542]

Loss of Integrity = Unauthorized modification or destruction of information
Examples of Integrity Loss:
  • Financial records altered maliciously
  • System configurations changed without authorization
  • Data corrupted during transmission
  • Audit logs tampered with or deleted
‚ö° Availability ‚ñ∂
Full Definition:

"Ensuring timely and reliable access to and use of information" [44 U.S.C., SEC. 3542]

Loss of Availability = Disruption of access to or use of information or an information system
Examples of Availability Loss:
  • Denial of Service (DoS) attacks
  • System crashes preventing access
  • Network outages
  • Ransomware encrypting critical files

Important Concept: Different Information Needs Different Protection

Not all information requires the same level of protection for each objective:

  • Public website: Low confidentiality needs, high integrity and availability needs
  • Classified research: High confidentiality needs, moderate integrity and availability
  • Financial transactions: High needs for all three objectives
üí° Key Understanding: Every piece of information and every system must be evaluated against all three objectives. The level of protection needed for each may differ significantly.
Impact Levels: Low, Moderate, and High Part 3 of 6

üìä Understanding Potential Impact

Core Concept

FIPS 199 defines three levels of potential impact if there's a breach of security (loss of confidentiality, integrity, or availability). These must be considered within each organization's context and the overall national interest.

üîç The Three Impact Levels Explained

LOW

Limited Adverse Effect

Definition: The loss could be expected to have a LIMITED adverse effect on organizational operations, assets, or individuals.

Examples of LOW Impact ‚ñ∂
  • Degraded mission capability but organization can still perform primary functions (effectiveness noticeably reduced)
  • Minor damage to organizational assets
  • Minor financial loss
  • Minor harm to individuals
Real-world example: Temporary unavailability of an employee directory system - inconvenient but doesn't stop operations.
MODERATE

Serious Adverse Effect

Definition: The loss could be expected to have a SERIOUS adverse effect on organizational operations, assets, or individuals.

Examples of MODERATE Impact ‚ñ∂
  • Significant degradation in mission capability (can perform but effectiveness significantly reduced)
  • Significant damage to organizational assets
  • Significant financial loss
  • Significant harm to individuals (not life-threatening)
Real-world example: Breach of procurement system exposing bid information - causes significant financial and reputational damage.
HIGH

Severe or Catastrophic Adverse Effect

Definition: The loss could be expected to have a SEVERE or CATASTROPHIC adverse effect on organizational operations, assets, or individuals.

Examples of HIGH Impact ‚ñ∂
  • Severe degradation or loss of mission capability (unable to perform primary functions)
  • Major damage to organizational assets
  • Major financial loss
  • Severe or catastrophic harm to individuals (including loss of life)
Real-world example: Compromise of a power grid control system - could cause widespread outages and potential loss of life.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Note on Individual Harm

Adverse effects on individuals may include loss of privacy entitled under law. This is why personal information often requires higher protection levels.

Categorizing Information Types Part 4 of 6

üìÅ Security Categories for Information

What is an Information Type?

A specific category of information (e.g., privacy, medical, proprietary, financial, investigative, contractor sensitive, security management) defined by an organization or by law/regulation.

üìù The Categorization Formula

SCinformation type = {(confidentiality, impact), (integrity, impact), (availability, impact)}

Where impact values are: LOW, MODERATE, HIGH, or NOT APPLICABLE*

*NOT APPLICABLE only applies to confidentiality (public information)

üìö Real Examples from FIPS 199

Example 1: Public Web Server Information ‚ñ∂

Scenario:

An organization managing public information on its web server determines:

  • NO potential impact from loss of confidentiality (it's public)
  • MODERATE impact from loss of integrity (wrong info could mislead)
  • MODERATE impact from loss of availability (public expects access)
Result:
SCpublic information = {(confidentiality, NA), (integrity, MODERATE), (availability, MODERATE)}
Insight: Even public information needs protection - not for confidentiality, but for integrity and availability!
Example 2: Law Enforcement Investigative Information ‚ñ∂

Scenario:

Law enforcement managing extremely sensitive investigative information determines:

  • HIGH impact from loss of confidentiality (could compromise investigations)
  • MODERATE impact from loss of integrity (evidence tampering concerns)
  • MODERATE impact from loss of availability (investigations could continue with delays)
Result:
SCinvestigative information = {(confidentiality, HIGH), (integrity, MODERATE), (availability, MODERATE)}
Insight: Different objectives can have different impact levels based on the nature of the information.
Example 3: Routine Administrative Information ‚ñ∂

Scenario:

Financial organization managing routine administrative information (non-privacy) determines:

  • LOW impact from loss of confidentiality
  • LOW impact from loss of integrity
  • LOW impact from loss of availability
Result:
SCadministrative information = {(confidentiality, LOW), (integrity, LOW), (availability, LOW)}
Insight: Not all information needs high protection - appropriate categorization prevents over-protecting low-value information.

Categorizing Information Systems Part 5 of 6

üñ•Ô∏è From Information to Systems

The High Water Mark Principle

An information system's security category is determined by the HIGHEST impact level (high water mark) among all information types it processes, stores, or transmits.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Difference from Information Types

"NOT APPLICABLE" is never valid for systems!

Every system has a minimum LOW impact because:

  • System processes themselves need protection
  • System information (passwords, configurations) requires security
  • Loss of system functions affects operations

üìä System Categorization Examples

Example 4: Acquisition System ‚ñ∂

System Contents:

A contracting organization's acquisition system contains:

  1. Sensitive contract information:
    SC = {(C: MODERATE), (I: MODERATE), (A: LOW)}
  2. Routine administrative information:
    SC = {(C: LOW), (I: LOW), (A: LOW)}
High Water Mark Calculation:
Confidentiality: MAX(MODERATE, LOW) = MODERATE
Integrity: MAX(MODERATE, LOW) = MODERATE
Availability: MAX(LOW, LOW) = LOW

System Category:
SCacquisition system = {(confidentiality, MODERATE), (integrity, MODERATE), (availability, LOW)}
Key Point: The system must protect at the level of its most sensitive information type.
Example 5: SCADA System (With Adjustment) ‚ñ∂

System Contents:

Power plant SCADA system controlling military installation contains:

  1. Real-time sensor data:
    SC = {(C: NA), (I: HIGH), (A: HIGH)}
  2. Administrative information:
    SC = {(C: LOW), (I: LOW), (A: LOW)}
Initial High Water Mark:
Confidentiality: MAX(NA‚ÜíLOW, LOW) = LOW
Integrity: MAX(HIGH, LOW) = HIGH
Availability: MAX(HIGH, LOW) = HIGH

Initial System Category:
SC = {(C: LOW), (I: HIGH), (A: HIGH)}
Management Adjustment:
Organization increases confidentiality from LOW to MODERATE because unauthorized access to system controls could reveal vulnerabilities.
Final System Category:
SCSCADA system = {(confidentiality, MODERATE), (integrity, HIGH), (availability, HIGH)}
Lesson: Organizations can adjust categories upward based on system-level considerations.

Remember System-Level Information

Systems contain critical operational information that requires protection:

  • Network routing tables
  • Password files
  • Cryptographic keys
  • Security configurations
  • Audit logs

These must be protected at least at the level of the most critical user information!

üí° Key Understanding: System categorization uses the "high water mark" principle - the highest impact level for each objective across ALL information types determines the system's security category.

FIPS 199 provides a standardized method for categorizing federal information and systems based on impact to confidentiality, integrity, and availability.

üîÑ The Categorization Process

Step-by-Step Process:

  1. Identify Information Types: What kinds of information do you have?
  2. Assess Impact per Type: For each type, evaluate C-I-A impacts
  3. Document Categories: Use the SC notation format
  4. System Categorization: Apply high water mark across all types
  5. Review and Adjust: Consider system-level factors
  6. Apply Controls: Use category to determine security requirements
üîó Connection to Other Standards ‚ñ∂

FIPS 199 categorization leads to:

  • FIPS 200: Minimum security requirements for each category
  • NIST SP 800-53: Specific security controls based on category
  • NIST SP 800-60: Guide for mapping information types
  • Risk Management Framework: Category drives control selection

The security category is the foundation for ALL subsequent security decisions!

‚öñÔ∏è Key Considerations ‚ñ∂

When Categorizing, Remember:

  • Context Matters: Consider your organization's mission
  • Err on Caution: When in doubt, choose higher category
  • Document Rationale: Record why you chose each level
  • Regular Review: Categories can change over time
  • Aggregate Risk: Multiple LOW items together might equal MODERATE
  • Compliance: Some regulations mandate minimum levels
