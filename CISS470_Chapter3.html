üìã COBIT 5 GEIT: Five Governing Principles ‚ñ∂

COBIT 5 GEIT (Governance of Enterprise Information Technology) provides five principles:

  1. Meeting stakeholder needs - Aligning objectives with stakeholder requirements
  2. Covering the enterprise end-to-end - Managing IT as a business asset
  3. Applying a single, integrated framework - Consistent guidance across the organization
  4. Enabling a holistic approach - Considering all interacting components
  5. Separating governance from management - Clear distinction between oversight and execution

Key Point: These principles focus on HOW to govern and manage enterprise IT effectively.

üéØ ISO 31000: Eleven Risk Management Principles ‚ñ∂

ISO 31000:2009 Risk Management provides principles that guide behavior:

  1. Risk management is transparent and inclusive
  2. Risk management is dynamic, iterative, and responsive to change
  3. Risk management creates and protects value
  4. Risk management is tailored
  5. Risk management explicitly addresses uncertainty
  6. Risk management is systematic, structured, and timely
  7. Risk management is an integral part of all organizational processes
  8. Risk management takes human and cultural factors into account
  9. Risk management is part of decision making
  10. Risk management is based on the best available information
  11. Risk management facilitates continual improvement

Important Note: The word "cyber" is inserted in the text to emphasize IT-specific risk, but these principles apply across the entire organization.

COBIT Principle 1: Meeting Stakeholder Needs Part 2 of 5

ü§ù Meeting Stakeholder Needs Through Transparency and Responsiveness

The First COBIT 5 Principle

This principle "addresses the need to align individual and departmental objectives and priorities with enterprise and stakeholder needs." It recognizes that stakeholder needs and enterprise goals change over time.

üåê Being Transparent and Inclusive

ISO 31000 Principle: "Risk management is transparent and inclusive"

What it means: "Appropriate and timely involvement of stakeholders and, in particular, decision makers at all levels ensures that [cyber] risk management remains relevant and up-to-date."

Examples of Cyber Risk Stakeholders:

  • Customers, clients, stockholders
  • Employees, contractors, supply chain partners
  • Outsourced partners and critical infrastructure suppliers
  • Government and regulatory authorities
  • Nongovernmental organizations
  • Civil society groups
  • Members of the public (including media)

Key Questions to Answer:

  • "What is each stakeholder expecting from the organization when it comes to managing cyber risk?"
  • "What are the regulations that apply to digital information and sensitive data?"
  • "What voluntary or contractual obligations exist for network, systems, and data?"

Remember: The answers may differ depending on the stakeholder!

üîÑ Being Responsive to Change

ISO 31000 Principle: "Risk management is dynamic, iterative, and responsive to change"

Core Message: "[Cyber] risk management continually senses and responds to change. As external and internal events occur, context and knowledge change, monitoring and review of risks take place, new risks emerge, some change, and others disappear."

Why This Matters for Cyber:

Given the disruptive nature of technology and speed of change, this principle addresses the intersection between:

  • Stakeholders with different (sometimes conflicting) needs
  • Changes in internal and external circumstances
  • Technology refreshes and updates
  • Operational process changes
  • New applications/software solutions
  • Evolution in how stakeholders access and use systems

Creating Both Opportunities and Threats:

Opportunities
  • Innovation enablement
  • Competitive advantage
  • Efficiency gains
  • New capabilities
Threats
  • New vulnerabilities
  • Attack surface expansion
  • Complexity risks
  • Integration challenges

üîç How to Demonstrate These Principles ‚ñ∂

Transparency and Inclusiveness is Demonstrated When:

  • The organization can clearly articulate each stakeholder's expectations
  • All relevant parties are involved in risk discussions
  • Communication channels are open and two-way
  • Stakeholder feedback is actively sought and incorporated

Responsiveness to Change is Demonstrated When:

  • Uncertainties and changes that modify IT assets are integrated into change management
  • Risk assessments are updated as circumstances evolve
  • New technologies trigger risk reviews
  • Stakeholder need changes prompt process adjustments
  • Formal and informal change processes incorporate risk considerations

COBIT Principles 2 & 3: Enterprise Coverage & Integration Part 3 of 5

üè¢ Covering the Enterprise End-to-End & Applying a Single Framework

COBIT Principle 2: Enterprise-Wide Perspective

Managing IT as an asset is essential for business value creation, covering "all functions and processes within the enterprise." Accountability rests with business managers, not IT functional roles.

üíé Three Supporting ISO Principles for Enterprise Coverage

1. Creating and Protecting Value

Principle: "Risk management creates and protects value"

Focuses on [cyber] risk management contributing to "demonstrable achievement of objectives and improvement of performance."

Demonstrated When:

  • Business managers (not just IT) consider uncertainties related to IT assets
  • Decisions include recognition of organizational value to be gained
  • Risk considerations include value protection aspects
  • IT investments are evaluated for business value creation
Critical Shift: IT assets are business assets, managed by business managers for business value.
2. Risk Management is Tailored

Principle: "[Cyber] risk management is aligned with the organization's external and internal context and risk profile"

Acknowledges Differences In:

  • Entity's operations
  • Stakeholder composition
  • Business environment
  • Industry requirements
  • Geographic considerations

Demonstrated Through:

  • Assessment methodologies customized to circumstances
  • Decisions based on proprietary knowledge
  • Actions tailored to specific risk sets
  • No "one-size-fits-all" approaches
3. Explicitly Addressing Uncertainty

Core Concept: People acknowledge that the future can be different from the past

The Principle Recognizes:

  • Not everything can be known
  • Circumstances change unpredictably
  • Ambiguity requires planning
  • Enterprises must adapt to unpredictable environments

Demonstrated Through:

  • Assessment methodologies examining potential factors
  • Scanning for emerging issues
  • Environmental change monitoring
  • Scenario planning exercises
  • Management action planning for various outcomes

COBIT Principle 3: Single, Integrated Framework

Using an overarching framework incorporating relevant standards (including ISO 31000) provides "consistent and integrated source of guidance" for effective GEIT.

üìê Being Systematic, Structured, and Timely

üéØ The Structured Approach Principle ‚ñ∂

ISO 31000 Principle: "Risk management is systematic, structured, and timely"

What This Means:

"A systematic, timely and structured approach to [cyber] risk management contributes to efficiency and to consistent, comparable and reliable results."

Key Requirements:

  • Management-directed criteria: Clear standards set by leadership
  • Replicable metrics: Consistent measurement approaches
  • Repeatable processes: Can be used whenever/wherever needed
  • Alignment with non-IT: Same approach for cyber and other risks

ISO 31000 Integration Points in COBIT 5:

  1. Evaluate, Direct, and Monitor - Governance activities
  2. Align, Plan, and Organize - Management activities
Success Indicator: The enterprise establishes and communicates a clear, naturally integrated way of dealing with risk that is appropriate for business management AND applicable to governing enterprise IT.

üîÑ Four Critical ISO Principles for Holistic Approach

1Ô∏è‚É£ Integral Part of All Organizational Processes ‚ñ∂

Core Message: "[Cyber] risk management is not a stand-alone activity separate from main activities. It's part of management responsibilities and integral to ALL organizational processes."

Integration Points:

  • Strategic planning: Risk considerations in strategy formation
  • Project management: Risk assessment in all projects
  • Change management: Risk evaluation for all changes
  • Daily operations: Risk awareness in routine activities

Natural Intersection for Cyber Risk:

Since all activities involve risk, risk management becomes the natural intersection for:

  • Legal and compliance functions
  • Human resources processes
  • Operational procedures
  • IT management and protection
  • Information and physical security
  • Privacy requirements
Demonstrated By: People working collaboratively across organizational systems to influence people, processes, and technology in managing cyber risks.
2Ô∏è‚É£ Taking Human and Cultural Factors Into Account ‚ñ∂

Principle: "Risk management recognizes the capabilities, perceptions, and intentions of external and internal people that can facilitate or hinder achievement of objectives."

External Uncertainties to Evaluate:

  • Disruptive consumer preferences
  • Behavior of industry participants
  • Supply chain partner actions
  • Competing inventions and innovations

Internal Uncertainties to Consider:

  • Innovation capacity
  • Ethical behavior alignment
  • Employee motivations
  • Cultural consistency with expectations

Key Questions Organizations Must Answer:

  • "Is this project consistent with cultural expectations for managing cyber risk?"
  • "Will people behave in the way we anticipate?"
  • "If not, what alternatives can reduce negative effects and increase positive effects?"

Remember: Answers differ depending on the project, initiative, or individuals involved.

COBIT Principle 5: Separating Governance from Management Part 5 of 5

üé≠ Separating Governance from Management & Continual Improvement

The Fifth COBIT Principle

This principle makes a clear distinction between governance and management activities, creating a repeatable, closed-loop system where management feedback ensures alignment with governance direction.

üèõÔ∏è Governance vs Management Activities ‚ñ∂

Governance Activities

Based on business needs:

  • Evaluate: Assess options and priorities
  • Direct: Set direction and objectives
  • Monitor: Track performance and compliance

Performed by: Board and committees

Management Activities

Execute governance direction:

  • Plan: Develop strategies and tactics
  • Build: Implement capabilities
  • Run: Execute operations
  • Monitor: Track operational performance

Performed by: Executive management

The Closed-Loop System:

  1. Governance sets direction based on business needs
  2. Management executes according to direction
  3. Management provides feedback on performance
  4. Governance evaluates and adjusts direction
  5. Cycle repeats continuously

üìà Maturity Strategy and Continual Improvement

ISO 31000 Principle: "Risk management facilitates continual improvement"

Core Requirement: "Organizations should develop and implement strategies to improve their [cyber] risk management maturity alongside all other aspects of their organization."

The Maturity Journey:

  1. Use processes: People apply risk management techniques
  2. Gain insights: Understanding of uncertainties improves
  3. Weigh alternatives: Better decision-making capability
  4. Make decisions: More informed choices
  5. Take beneficial actions: Improved outcomes
  6. Build capabilities: Natural maturity progression
  7. Apply consistently: Principles become embedded

Natural Alignment:

Continual improvement driven by risk maturity strategy aligns naturally with the separation of governance and management because:

  • Governance sets maturity targets
  • Management implements improvements
  • Feedback loops drive advancement
  • IT capabilities naturally improve
Ultimate Demonstration: Cyber risk management is embedded not only in IT-related governance and management activities, but in ALL governance and management activities of the enterprise.
