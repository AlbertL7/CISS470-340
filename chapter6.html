üéØ Putting Standards and Frameworks in Context

There are a multitude of cybersecurity standards in existence today developed by various bodies addressing specific needs. The list continues to grow, but it's important to identify those that bring the most value to your organization. Aligning to the "right" standards helps facilitate sharing and transparency on the most recent cyber attacks within the industry and beyond.

Diversity as a Blessing and Curse

According to Merriam-Webster's Dictionary, a framework is "the basic structure of something." That underlying something can be fairly diverse‚Äîfor instance, ideas, concepts, guidelines, rules, check lists, requirements, facts, or physical parts. And, in this context, diversity may be a blessing and a curse.

There are a large number of cybersecurity and IT risk management frameworks issued by technology vendors, professional services firms, public institutions, nonprofit organizations, and public-private partnerships‚Äîall providing a different focus:

üìç Geographic Scope

Local, regional, and global frameworks

üè¢ Industry Specificity

Generic and industry-specific frameworks

üéØ Strategic Approach

Value-focused and threat-focused frameworks

‚öôÔ∏è Technical Depth

Very technical frameworks for technical aspects of cybersecurity

üèõÔ∏è Governance

Governance and organizational frameworks

‚úÖ Assurance Types

Product assurance, process assurance, and environment assurance frameworks

üìã Compliance Focus

Compliance-focused frameworks for regulatory interactions

üìà Maturity Assessment

High-level maturity frameworks showing where you stand

üí° Best Practices

Collections of best practices to cover the basics or more

üîß Controls Focused

Built around inputs/ingredients for auditors

üöÄ Capability Focused

Aiming at outcomes, powerful but harder to assess

üîÑ Information Sharing

Focused on exchange and collaboration of cyber information

üåê Specialized vs Holistic

From specialized cybersecurity to holistic security covering IT, physical, people security, and cyber-physical systems

Obviously, all of these types of frameworks have their merits. Frameworks are a tool chest to structure thinking about, and acting on, security in a given context, and a given set of objectives.

No "Best" Cybersecurity Standard

Standards, as "a level of quality, achievement, etc., that is considered acceptable or desirable [...] established by authority, custom, or general consent as a model or example" (Merriam-Webster's), can augment frameworks. Standards formalize and serve as guiderails for cybersecurity. There is a similar breadth of standards as there is for frameworks.

This variety exists for a reason. As new technologies and delivery mechanisms develop, it will continue to accommodate change and expand to address fields such as digitalThe integration of digital technology into all areas of business, Internet of ThingsNetwork of physical devices embedded with sensors and connectivity, Big DataLarge, complex data sets requiring advanced processing capabilities, or simply the cloud. So there is no "best" cybersecurity standard or framework. But there are already many good tools for the job at hand‚Äîand less appropriate ones.

First Steps for CEO Tom

So where was CEO Tom to start? Before selecting a cybersecurity framework or standard, a first but important step is to clarify the organization's objectives or purpose regarding risks and issues to address or mitigate against.

The objectives may range from very operational tasks (e.g., configuration of employee computers) to daily governance issues (e.g., design of information security policy) and board-level responsibilities (e.g., ensuring executive team provides risk oversight). Other purposes may include:

  • üìö Establish common language and taxonomy - Allowing technical people, organization people, and risk managers to start communicating around cybersecurity
  • üîç Provide transparency - Assessing current state against a yardstick accepted by and understandable to the target audience (from technical experts to board members, customers to regulators)
  • üìã Provide guidelines for action - Against known gaps, threats, or identified areas for development
  • ‚úÖ Ensure and demonstrate compliance - With relevant regulation or laws, enable competition on security, or establish security beyond compliance
  • ‚öñÔ∏è Normalize cyber-related risk - Allowing it to be treated and included in enterprise risk management like any other organizational risk

üèóÔ∏è Commonly Used Frameworks and Standards

The following frameworks and standards are considered to outline globally accepted best practices.

ISO/IEC 27000 Family
‚ñº

This framework series, sometimes referred to as ISO 27k, covers a very broad series of topics. The framework is often considered the information security equivalent of ISO 9000 and provides certification options.

Issuer: International Organization for Standardization (ISO) and International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC), Geneva, Switzerland
Extent: Over 1,500 pages across the family; ISO 27001 is 23 pages (2013)
Region/Type: Global, international standard
Industry: All types of organizations and industries
Primary Audience: Information security, risk, and IT functions

Key Components:

  • ISO 27000: General vocabulary
  • ISO 27001: Requirements for information security management program
  • ISO 27002: Code of practice for information security management
  • ISO 27005: Information security risk management
  • ISO 27033: Network security guidance
  • ISO 27034: Application security
  • ISO 27799: Information security management in healthcare

Tom's Starting Point: ISO 27001 would be the best point to start‚Äîespecially if certification is a goal.

COBIT 5 for Information Security
‚ñº

COBIT is a comprehensive IT governance risk management framework especially suited for organizations accustomed to external auditing. It comprises process descriptions, implementation guidelines, and extensive descriptions of goals, controls, related metrics, and RACI suggestions.

Issuer: ISACA (Information Systems Audit and Control Association), United States
Extent: COBIT 5 and Information Security lens each ~220 pages (2012)
Region/Type: Global quasi-standard
Industry: All, especially financial services and highly regulated industries
Primary Audience: All stakeholders, especially information security, risk, and IT functions

Key Security Processes:

  • EDM #03: Ensure Risk Optimization
  • APO #013: Manage Security
  • DSS #05: Manage Security Services

Special Note: Very often used by firms to achieve compliance with Sarbanes-Oxley rules.

NIST Computer/Cybersecurity Frameworks
‚ñº

NIST uses three Special Publications (SP) subseries to publish guidelines, recommendations and reference materials. Also created a Cyber Security Framework for Critical Infrastructure.

Issuer: U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST)
Extent: Several thousand pages across repository
Region/Type: U.S. national standard, used globally
Industry: Often mandated in U.S. government context, applicable to all
Primary Audience: Information security, risk, IT functions, managers, auditors

Key Publications:

  • SP800 Series: Computer Security (current center of gravity)
  • SP800-171: Protection of controlled unclassified information
  • SP800-125B: Secure Virtual Network Configuration
  • SP800-82r2: Guide to Industrial Control Systems (ICS) security
  • SP800-100: Information Security Handbook for managers (180 pages, 2007)
  • SP800-53r4: Security and Privacy Controls (460 pages, 2015)
  • SP500 Series: Computer Systems Technology
  • SP1800 Series: NIST Cyber Security Practice Guide (since 2015)
  • Cybersecurity Framework: Critical Infrastructure (40 pages, 2014)

Tom's Starting Point: SP800-53r4 for auditable plans.

ISF Standard of Good Practice for Information Security
‚ñº

The most comprehensive information security standard in the world, providing more coverage than ISO. It covers security governance, security requirements, controls, monitoring/improvement and addresses risk from people, processes, and technology.

Issuer: Information Security Forum (ISF), United Kingdom
Extent: About 300 pages (2011 version), last updated 2014
Region/Type: Global quasi-standard with member chapters worldwide
Industry: Large organizations from public and private sector
Primary Audience: Information security, risk, IT audit functions, organization and IT managers

Key Features: Broader and more prescriptive than ISO. Enables compliance with ISO27001/2, COBIT 5, SANS Top 20, UK Cyber Essentials Scheme, and NIST Cyber Security Framework. Accompanied by tools and benchmark offerings for ISF members.

SANS Top 20 Critical Security Controls
‚ñº

The CIS Critical Security Controls form deliberately not a complete framework, but a widely adopted list of the top 20 specific and actionable cyber defense controls, based on NIST framework and regularly updated industry intelligence.

Issuer: SANS Institute (Escal Institute of Advanced Technologies, Inc.)/Center for Internet Security, USA
Extent: 94 pages, last updated 2016
Region/Type: Global quasi-standard
Industry: All
Primary Audience: Information security, risk, and IT functions

Coverage Areas: Inventory of devices and software, malware defense, secure configuration, wireless access control, incident response and management, penetration testing.

Created By: NSA Red and Blue teams, US Department of Energy nuclear energy labs, law enforcement organizations, and top forensics and incident response organizations‚Äîto answer "what do we need to do to stop known attacks."

For Tom: An excellent first step towards his supervisory board meeting, allowing him to structure and communicate his intent.

IT-CMF:ISM (IT Capability Maturity Framework)
‚ñº

Developed by practitioners and academics from a large consortium, aiming to become the gold standard for management of IT value and IT-enabled innovation. Designed around 35 IT capabilities rather than processes or specific controls.

Issuer: Innovation Value Institute (IVI), Ireland
Extent: Ten pages plus extensive accompanying materials, last updated 2014
Region/Type: Global, capability framework
Industry: Any
Primary Audience: Information Security and IT management functions

Unique Approach: Provides maturity assessment methodology, benchmarks, practices, outcomes, and metrics (POMs). Information security is treated as one of 35 IT capabilities. Moves beyond controls to an organization value-focused approach.

PCI-DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard)
‚ñº

De facto standard for protection of credit card account data, widely adopted in financial services and retail. Addresses six objectives through 12 actionable key requirements.

Issuer: Payment Card Industry (PCI) Security Standards Council, USA
Version: 3.2, about 140 pages, last updated 2016
Region/Type: Global industry standard
Industry: Financial services, retail, card data processing industries
Primary Audience: Information security and IT functions

Six Core Objectives: From "Build and Maintain a Secure Network and Systems" to "Maintain an Information Security Policy"

Compliance Note: Mandated by law in some countries. Most credit and payment card issuers require merchants and service providers to comply. If Tom's company processes card data, someone in the organization likely knows this standard.

World Economic Forum Cyber Risk Framework (WEF-CRF)
‚ñº

Provides a holistic high-level approach to addressing and calculating risk posed by cyber attacks. Looks at value at risk, potential attacker profiles, and organizational maturity.

Issuer: The World Economic Forum, Geneva, Switzerland
Extent: Twenty pages plus supporting reports, last updated 2015
Region/Type: Global framework
Industry: Any
Primary Audience: Information security, risk, and IT functions

Unique Features: "Cyber value-at-risk" framework that unifies all dimensions of cyber threats. Encourages organizations to create robust cyber risk models. Helps increase confidence regarding decisions to invest, distribute, offload and/or retain cyber risks.

Board Focus: WEF is developing Cyber Resilience Guidelines for Boards to normalize cyber risk from a supervisory board perspective.

ENISA (European Union Agency for Network and Information Security)
‚ñº

Currently focusing on critical infrastructure protection and national cybersecurity strategies of member states, while paying attention to SME cybersecurity needs.

Issuer: European Union Agency for Network and Information Security, Greece
Extent: Several specific publications available
Region/Type: European Union
Industry: Government agencies, national critical infrastructure, SMEs
Primary Audience: Information security, risk, and IT functions

Key Publications:

  • "Evaluation Framework on National Cyber Security Strategies" (12/2014)
  • "Information Security and Privacy Standards for SMEs" (12/2015)

Mission: "Securing Europe's Information Society" - Contributing to network and information security culture for citizens, consumers, enterprises and public sector organizations.

Note: ENISA advocates use of existing frameworks rather than creating new ones.

‚úÖ Tailoring Your Framework Choice

Since there are many frameworks and standards available, and they typically are largely compatible at the core but differentiated at the fringes, organizations often benefit from an informed combination of several frameworks to best match their particular need and tailored to their objectives, context, and risk profile.

‚ö†Ô∏è Exception: If one framework is required by regulation or particular key customers, that becomes your baseline. For multinationals operating in several jurisdictions, using more than one framework, or complying with more than one standard, may not even be a choice but a must.

Decision Factors for Framework Selection

  • ‚úì Regulatory Requirements: What's mandated in your jurisdiction?
  • ‚úì Customer Expectations: What do your key customers require?
  • ‚úì Industry Standards: What's common in your sector?
  • ‚úì Organizational Maturity: Where are you in your security journey?
  • ‚úì Resources Available: What can you sustainably implement with skilled internal resources?
  • ‚úì Business Strategy: Compliance vs. security beyond compliance? Partnering vs. competing on security?
  • ‚úì Geographic Scope: Single country or multinational operations?
  • ‚úì Economic Feasibility: What's economically sustainable for your organization?

Tom's Recommended Approach

So out of the plethora of cybersecurity frameworks and standards, which ones should Tom consider at a minimum? Given the tight deadline for his board presentation, Tom would probably be well advised to:

  1. First Step: Run an IT-CMF:ISM assessment or any other enterprise-wide focused cybersecurity health check
  2. Quick Wins: Explore the SANS Top 20 for immediate actionable controls and structure to communicate his intent
  3. Strategic Selection: Take a step back for a more informed pick among the other, more comprehensive frameworks
  4. Board Preparation: With more time, familiarize himself with the upcoming Cyber Resilience Guidelines for Boards the World Economic Forum is currently developing‚Äîbecause this may well be what the board members who requested his presentation will use to gauge his preparedness

Implementation Approach

Real security comes from a structured approach:

1. Strategy Decision

Decide together within the organization on appropriate security strategy and overall objectives

2. Framework Adoption

Adopt and adapt framework to situation, enrich with practices, augment with relevant elements

3. Risk Assessment

Run comprehensive risk assessment to identify gaps and priorities

4. Roadmap Creation

Build roadmap for implementation of cybersecurity/cyber risk management system

5. Capability Building

Establish required capabilities to keep system functioning, monitored, and up to date

6. Quick Fixes

Prioritize and close obvious or previously identified gaps quickly

‚ö†Ô∏è Constraints on Standards and Frameworks

These are but a selection of the most important frameworks and standards. Although there are more and different ones‚Äîcreated for different purposes, industries, audiences, or specific regions‚Äîit is key to remember that all of these can have their merit if employed as a tool for the right purpose.

Likewise, it is important to keep in mind that risk methods and frameworks may be affected by some constraints and fundamental limitations.

Key Limitations (per UK CESG/GCHQ)

These limitations are nicely summarized in an article by the UK Communications Electronics Security Group (CESG), the information security arm of the GCHQ:

  • Limits in a "reductionist approach" - Breaking down complex systems may miss emergent properties
  • Lack of variety - Limited perspectives in framework design
  • Limits of a "fixed-state" approach - Static frameworks in dynamic environments
  • Lack of feedback and control - Missing continuous improvement mechanisms
  • Danger of losing risk signals in "security noise" - Important risks may be overlooked
  • Assumed determinability - Assuming predictable outcomes in complex systems

  • üë• Skilled Internal Resources - Dedicated security professionals and trained staff

The Path Forward

Usually, any framework needs to be adapted somewhat to the situation at hand and enriched with practices as needed, or augmented with relevant elements from other frameworks. Indeed, many companies follow such an approach.

A next step would be to:

  1. Run a risk assessment
  2. Build a roadmap for implementation of a cybersecurity/cyber risk management system
  3. Establish the required capabilities to keep all of this functioning, monitored, and up to date

Obviously, it is prudent to prioritize and close obvious or already previously identified gaps quickly, rather than waiting for the end of a more comprehensive cybersecurity transformation project.
